The bench press is a major compound movement that engages the Pectorals, Anterior Deltoids, and the Triceps. It also uses stabilizers in the upper back, core, and shoulders. If done correctly, the legs also have minor engagement to maintain full body tension. It is one of the more famous exercises performed in the gym, and many people's favorite. The positioning of the hands on the bar can change how much different muscle groups are engaged. Wider hands will typically engage more chest, while narrower hands will prioritize triceps. In this exercise, you want to keep your arms from flaring out too wide, as that can be damaging to the shoulders. Normally, a 45 to 60 degree angle between the arm and body is optimal to place the load on the chest rather than the shoulders. You also want to drive your heels into the ground and push yourself up the bench. Oftentimes, people will arch their back to create more tension along the front of the body, as well as force your arms to stay closer to the torso. Some cues that I find helpful to improve my bench press are to try to bend the bar, which maintains the arm angle that I mentioned before. I also try to push my shoulder blades together and down, as this will maintain tension in the upper back to stabilize. I use a slight arch when I bench, but beginners can oftenttimes have issues with maintaining a healthy arch, so I would recommend making sure you know what you're doing before you use it. Another technique that can be useful in building a big bench is the Valsalva Maneuver. This is a technique to engage the core by creating pressure within the abdomen.
